The Last Train Home

The station was almost empty when Elias arrived.

A single flickering light buzzed overhead, casting long shadows across the cracked concrete. The old clock on the wall read 11:58 PM. Two minutes until the last train.

He adjusted the strap on his bag and glanced down the tracks. Nothing yet. Just darkness stretching into more darkness.

“Cutting it close, aren’t you?”

Elias turned. An old woman sat on the bench behind him. He hadn’t noticed her before.

“Yeah,” he said. “Didn’t think I’d make it.”

“They always come,” she replied calmly. “The trains, I mean. Whether we’re ready or not.”

Elias gave a small laugh. “I guess that’s one way to look at it.”

Silence settled between them, thick but not uncomfortable.

“Where are you headed?” she asked.

“Home,” Elias said quickly. Then, after a pause, “At least… I think so.”

The woman nodded, like she understood more than he’d said.

“Funny thing about home,” she murmured. “Sometimes it isn’t a place you go back to. Sometimes it’s a place you finally decide to go to.”

Elias frowned slightly. “What does that mean?”

But the woman didn’t answer. Instead, she looked down the tracks, her eyes reflecting something distant.

A low rumble began to grow.

The train.

Its headlights cut through the darkness, bright and sudden. The ground trembled as it approached, metal screeching against metal.

Elias stepped forward, gripping his bag tighter. His heart raced—not from the run, but from something else. Something heavier.

The train slowed, then stopped with a sharp hiss.

Doors slid open.

Warm light spilled out.

Elias hesitated.

“You’re not getting on?” he asked, turning back to the bench.

But the woman was gone.

Only the faint indentation in the seat remained.

“Guess it’s just me,” he whispered.

The conductor leaned out slightly. “You boarding?”

Elias looked into the train. People sat quietly, some staring ahead, others lost in thought. No one looked back at him.

He thought about where he had come from.

The missed calls. The unfinished conversations. The things he had said—and the things he hadn’t.

Then he thought about where he could go.

A deep breath.

“Yeah,” he said.

He stepped inside.

The doors closed behind him with a soft finality.

As the train pulled away, Elias felt something shift—not around him, but within him.

For the first time in a long while…

He wasn’t running.

He was going.
